\begin{abstract}
Long-running autonomous agents face a practical reliability challenge:
as sessions extend over hundreds of sequential turns, models may
progressively narrow their effective decision-making to a subset of the
factors they were instructed to monitor---even while continuing to
\emph{write about} all of them. We introduce \textbf{CoherenceBench}, a
benchmark that measures this phenomenon, which we term \emph{attention
collapse}, in a controlled power grid control room scenario with six
interdependent subsystems. The benchmark presents agents with 200
sequential decision ticks and measures five metrics spanning format
compliance and behavioral accuracy. Our central finding is that format
compliance (Factor Coverage) can remain at 1.00 throughout a session
while Decision Accuracy diverges dramatically across models: Claude
Haiku degrades from 68\% DA in early ticks to 18\% by session end (50
percentage points of temporal degradation), whereas GPT-5.4 maintains
stable DA ($\sim$33\%) with no measurable degradation. This divergence
is invisible to format-only evaluation. We further observe that
degradation in Claude Haiku is \emph{directional}: the model fixates on
early-phase anomaly factors (Load, Generation) at the expense of
late-phase factors (Weather, Reserve), producing a +38\% directional
fixation gap. Explicit intervention reminders reduce this gap from +42\%
to +14\% but do not eliminate it. Our results suggest that attention
collapse is a model-dependent, measurable phenomenon that standard
benchmarks do not capture and that agent developers should account for in
long-horizon deployments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

Large language models are increasingly deployed as long-running
autonomous agents tasked with monitoring, analysis, and decision-making
over extended time horizons \cite{park2023generative,
significant2023auto}. Industrial applications range from infrastructure
monitoring to continuous code review to multi-step scientific workflows.
While single-turn and few-turn benchmarks extensively evaluate model
capabilities \cite{jimenez2024swebench, zhou2024webarena}, the behavior
of these models over hundreds of sequential turns remains underexplored.

We observe a practical failure mode in long-running agent deployments: as
session length increases, models tend to progressively narrow their
\emph{effective} analysis, attending to fewer of the factors they were
explicitly instructed to monitor. We call this phenomenon \emph{attention
collapse}. Critically, this collapse is not visible at the format level.
An agent experiencing attention collapse may continue to write
structured, well-formatted responses that mention all required factors.
But its \emph{decisions}---the actions it recommends---reveal that it is
no longer genuinely processing factors where anomalies have shifted.

This matters for safety-critical deployments. A power grid operator, an
autonomous monitoring agent, or a medical triage system that writes about
all relevant subsystems but only acts on a subset creates a false sense
of coverage. The failure mode is insidious precisely because it passes
format-level validation. Gartner projects that by 2028, 40\% of agentic
AI projects will be abandoned due to compounding errors
\cite{gartner2025agents}---attention collapse may be one mechanism
driving such abandonment.

Recent work on agent drift \cite{agentdrift2025} documents goal
degradation in long-horizon agents, and the Engram project
\cite{engram2026} identifies coherence ceilings in multi-turn sessions.
CoherenceBench complements these efforts by providing a controlled,
reproducible benchmark with behavioral metrics that separate what an
agent \emph{says} from what it \emph{does}.

Our key findings are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Format compliance (FC = 1.00) is maintained by all models
    tested throughout 200-tick sessions, meaning format-only evaluation
    cannot detect attention collapse.
    \item Decision Accuracy (DA) diverges dramatically across model
    families: Claude Haiku degrades by 50 percentage points over 200
    ticks while GPT-5.4 shows approximately zero degradation.
    \item Degradation is \emph{directional}: Claude Haiku fixates on
    factors where anomalies were historically frequent (early-phase
    Load/Generation) rather than where anomalies are currently occurring
    (late-phase Weather/Reserve).
    \item Explicit intervention reminders partially mitigate collapse
    (reducing the fixation gap from +42\% to +14\%) but do not eliminate
    it.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item We introduce CoherenceBench, the first benchmark measuring
    multi-factor coherence degradation over long sessions with behavioral
    metrics.
    \item We define five complementary metrics (FC, FI, DA, ADR, IR) that
    capture both format-level and behavior-level aspects of attention
    collapse, and demonstrate that format metrics alone are insufficient.
    \item We provide evidence of \emph{directional fixation}: models do
    not degrade uniformly but tend to fixate on historically anomalous
    factors.
    \item We evaluate mitigation strategies and report cross-model
    differences that suggest model-dependent coherence properties not
    captured by existing benchmarks.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Long-context benchmarks.}
Prior work on evaluating long-context models focuses primarily on
retrieval (needle-in-a-haystack), summarization, and question answering
over long documents \cite{kamradt2023needle, liu2024lost}. Liu et
al.~\cite{liu2024lost} demonstrate that models struggle to use
information placed in the middle of long contexts---a positional bias in
\emph{retrieval}. CoherenceBench measures a different phenomenon: not
whether a model can \emph{find} information in a long context, but
whether it maintains \emph{balanced multi-factor decision-making} across
sequential turns where the context grows incrementally. The distinction
is between static long-context comprehension and dynamic long-session
coherence.

\paragraph{Agent benchmarks.}
Existing agent benchmarks evaluate task completion but typically involve
short interaction horizons. SWE-bench \cite{jimenez2024swebench}
measures whether agents can resolve GitHub issues, and WebArena
\cite{zhou2024webarena} evaluates web navigation, but both focus on
single-task completion rather than sustained multi-factor monitoring.
Vending-Bench \cite{vendingbench2025} extends evaluation to longer-form
agent tasks but does not specifically measure coherence degradation over
time. CoherenceBench is, to our knowledge, the first benchmark
specifically designed to measure attention degradation over 200+
sequential decision turns with behavioral metrics.

\paragraph{Agent drift and coherence ceilings.}
The Agent Drift project \cite{agentdrift2025} documents goal degradation
in long-horizon language agents, showing that agents progressively lose
alignment with their original objectives. The Engram project
\cite{engram2026} identifies coherence ceilings---maximum session lengths
beyond which agent performance degrades sharply. CoherenceBench provides
a complementary lens: rather than measuring overall goal drift or
identifying a single ceiling, we measure the \emph{pattern} of
degradation across multiple factors simultaneously, revealing directional
fixation as a specific failure mode.

\paragraph{Cognitive biases in LLMs.}
A growing literature documents human-like cognitive biases in LLMs.
Tversky and Kahneman's foundational work on anchoring and availability
heuristics \cite{tversky1974judgment} has been shown to manifest in
language models \cite{jones2022capturing, hagendorff2023human}. The
directional fixation we observe in CoherenceBench is consistent with an
availability-like bias: models over-weight factors where anomalies were
historically frequent (and thus more ``available'' in the conversation
history). However, we note that this behavioral parallel does not
constitute evidence for shared cognitive mechanisms---the underlying
causes may be entirely different (e.g., attention weight distributions in
transformers \cite{vaswani2017attention}, recency/primacy effects in
context windows \cite{press2022train}).

\paragraph{Agent architectures.}
ReAct \cite{yao2023react} and Reflexion \cite{shinn2023reflexion}
introduce reasoning-and-acting loops and verbal reinforcement learning
for language agents. These architectures improve single-task performance
but do not specifically address multi-factor coherence over long
sessions. CoherenceBench could serve as an evaluation harness for
future agent architectures that claim to maintain balanced attention.

\section{Benchmark Design}
\label{sec:design}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Scenario: Power Grid Control Room}

CoherenceBench uses a simulated power grid control room as its test
scenario. We chose this domain because it requires genuinely simultaneous
monitoring of multiple independent subsystems---a single correct action
often depends on the state of two or more factors---and because the
domain is sufficiently specialized that models cannot rely on memorized
patterns from training data. The agent acts as an autonomous grid
operator receiving updates from six subsystems every tick:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Load} --- consumer demand across 3 geographic zones
    \item \textbf{Generation} --- power plant output levels and fault
    status
    \item \textbf{Frequency} --- grid stability measured in Hz
    (deviations from 50.00 Hz)
    \item \textbf{Voltage} --- transmission line voltage levels across
    the network
    \item \textbf{Weather} --- wind speed, solar irradiance, and
    forecast conditions
    \item \textbf{Reserve} --- battery storage levels, gas turbine
    availability, spinning reserve margin
\end{enumerate}

Each tick, the agent receives a structured status update for all 6
factors and must respond with (a) an analysis of each factor's current
state, (b) identification of any anomalous conditions, and (c) a
recommended action from a set of 10 possible actions (e.g.,
\texttt{shed\_load}, \texttt{start\_gas\_turbine},
\texttt{activate\_battery}, \texttt{curtail\_renewables},
\texttt{hold\_steady}).

\subsection{Anomaly Injection}
\label{sec:anomaly_injection}

Anomalies are injected with phase-dependent probabilities designed to
create an ``attention trap.'' The key design principle is that early-tick
anomalies concentrate in one set of factors and late-tick anomalies shift
to a different set, so an agent that fixates on historically frequent
anomaly sources will miss current ones.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Phase-dependent anomaly injection probabilities per tick. Early
factors (Load, Generation) have high anomaly rates in Phase 1 that
decrease in Phase 3. Late factors (Weather, Reserve) follow the reverse
pattern. Frequency and Voltage serve as stable baselines.}
\label{tab:anomaly_schedule}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Factor} & \textbf{Phase 1 (t=1--80)} & \textbf{Phase 2
(t=81--120)} & \textbf{Phase 3 (t=121--200)} \\
\midrule
Load          & 0.40 & 0.15 & 0.05 \\
Generation    & 0.35 & 0.15 & 0.05 \\
Frequency     & 0.10 & 0.10 & 0.10 \\
Voltage       & 0.10 & 0.10 & 0.10 \\
Weather       & 0.05 & 0.20 & 0.40 \\
Reserve       & 0.05 & 0.20 & 0.35 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Every 7th tick ($t = 7, 14, 21, \ldots$) is designated a
\emph{multi-factor tick} in which the ground-truth correct action
requires integrating information from 2 or more factors simultaneously
(e.g., high load combined with low reserve requires
\texttt{start\_gas\_turbine} rather than \texttt{shed\_load} alone).
These multi-factor ticks serve as stress tests for coherent multi-factor
reasoning.

Anomaly templates are parameterized (severity, duration, affected zones)
and drawn from a library of 48 anomaly types. Each anomaly type has a
pre-defined set of acceptable actions---typically 2--3 valid responses
per anomaly---so scoring is not brittle to a single ``correct'' answer.

\subsection{Ground Truth and Scoring}

For each tick, a ground-truth oracle defines the set of acceptable
actions given the current state of all 6 factors. An action is scored as
correct (DA = 1) if it falls within the acceptable set. This design
accommodates the reality that multiple reasonable responses exist for
most grid conditions.

The oracle is deterministic given the random seed: for a fixed seed, the
sequence of anomalies, their severities, and the acceptable action sets
are fully reproducible.

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

We define five complementary metrics that together distinguish
format-level compliance from behavioral accuracy:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{CoherenceBench metrics. FC and FI measure what the agent
\emph{writes}; DA and ADR measure what it \emph{does}. IR measures
mitigation durability.}
\label{tab:metrics}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lp{7cm}l@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Definition} & \textbf{Type} \\
\midrule
FC (Factor Coverage) & Fraction of the 6 factors that receive
substantive analysis (more than a boilerplate sentence) in the agent's
response & Format \\
FI (Fixation Index) & Fraction of the agent's analytical tokens devoted
to its single most-discussed factor; higher values indicate imbalanced
attention & Format \\
DA (Decision Accuracy) & Binary: 1 if the recommended action is within
the acceptable set, 0 otherwise & Behavioral \\
ADR (Anomaly Detection Rate) & Fraction of currently anomalous factors
for which the agent's response includes a specific, actionable mention of
the anomaly & Behavioral \\
IR (Intervention Recovery) & Number of ticks after an intervention before
DA drops below its pre-intervention level; measures mitigation durability
& Behavioral \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The critical distinction is between FC and DA. A model with FC = 1.00
and declining DA is \emph{writing about} all six factors but not
\emph{acting on} the correct ones---attention collapse that is invisible
to format-only evaluation.

\subsection{Directional Fixation}

We define the \emph{directional fixation gap} as the difference in DA
between early-phase factors (Load, Generation) and late-phase factors
(Weather, Reserve) during late ticks (Phase 3, $t = 121$--$200$):

\begin{equation}
\text{DFG} = \text{DA}_{\text{early factors, Phase 3}} -
\text{DA}_{\text{late factors, Phase 3}}
\label{eq:dfg}
\end{equation}

A positive DFG indicates that the model performs better on factors that
\emph{were} historically anomalous than on factors that \emph{are}
currently anomalous---the signature of directional fixation.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Models}

We evaluate two model families in our primary experiments:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Claude Haiku} (Anthropic) --- a smaller, faster model
    optimized for throughput
    \item \textbf{GPT-5.4} (OpenAI) --- a larger model from the GPT
    family
\end{itemize}

We selected these two models because they represent different points on
the capability--cost spectrum and because preliminary observations
suggested they might exhibit qualitatively different coherence profiles.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% ============================================================

\subsection{The Format--Behavior Dissociation}
\label{sec:format_behavior}

Our most striking finding is that format-level metrics completely fail to
predict behavioral degradation. Both Claude Haiku and GPT-5.4 maintain
Factor Coverage (FC) at or near 1.00 throughout all 200 ticks across all
seeds---every response mentions all 6 factors with substantive analysis.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/fig1_fc_vs_da_both_models.png}
    \caption{Factor Coverage (FC) vs. Decision Accuracy (DA) over 200
    ticks for Claude Haiku and GPT-5.4. FC remains at 1.00 for both
    models throughout the session. DA diverges dramatically: Claude Haiku
    degrades from $\sim$68\% to $\sim$18\%, while GPT-5.4 remains stable
    at $\sim$33\%. The shaded region represents the ``invisible collapse
    zone''---the gap between what format metrics report and what
    behavioral metrics reveal.}
    \label{fig:fc_vs_da}
\end{figure}

Yet Decision Accuracy tells a completely different story
(Figure~\ref{fig:fc_vs_da}). Claude Haiku begins at 68\% DA in the
first 40 ticks and degrades to 18\% DA in the final window---a temporal
degradation of 50 percentage points. GPT-5.4 maintains approximately
stable DA throughout, beginning at 32\% in the first 40 ticks and ending
at 33\% in the final window ($\sim$0 percentage points of degradation).

This dissociation is the core message of CoherenceBench: an agent can
\emph{write about} all factors correctly while \emph{acting on} only a
subset. Format compliance does not indicate genuine attention.

\label{sec:cross_model}

The two models exhibit fundamentally different coherence profiles:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Claude Haiku: high start, steep collapse.} DA starts
    strong (68\% in ticks 1--40) but degrades monotonically. By the
    final window, DA has fallen to 18\%---worse than a random baseline
    among the 10 possible actions. The degradation is not uniform across
    factors: early-phase factor DA (59\%) substantially exceeds
    late-phase factor DA (22\%), yielding a directional fixation gap of
    +38\%.

    \item \textbf{GPT-5.4: flat and stable.} DA hovers around 33--38\%
    throughout the session with no detectable temporal trend. The
    directional fixation gap is 0\%: the model performs equally well (or
    equally poorly) on early-phase and late-phase factors. GPT-5.4 never
    reaches Claude Haiku's peak performance but also never collapses.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Directional Fixation}
\label{sec:directional}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{figures/fig3_da_by_factor_phase.png}
    \caption{Decision Accuracy broken down by factor and phase for Claude
    Haiku. In Phase 3 (ticks 121--200), the model maintains reasonable
    DA for Load and Generation anomalies (which are now rare) but
    performs poorly on Weather and Reserve anomalies (which are now
    frequent). This asymmetry constitutes directional fixation.}
    \label{fig:da_by_factor}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:da_by_factor} decomposes Claude Haiku's DA by factor
and phase. The directional fixation pattern is clear: in Phase 3,
when Weather and Reserve anomalies dominate, Claude Haiku's DA on
these factors (22\%) is dramatically lower than its DA on Load and
Generation anomalies (59\%). The model is not simply ``getting tired''
or producing worse outputs overall---it is specifically failing on
factors where anomalies have \emph{shifted to}, while maintaining
performance on factors where anomalies \emph{used to be}.

GPT-5.4, by contrast, shows no directional fixation: its DA on
early-phase and late-phase factors is symmetric (38\% for both in
Phase 3), consistent with a model that processes all factors with
equal (if moderate) depth regardless of anomaly history.

This finding has practical implications. An agent exhibiting directional
fixation will perform well during initial deployment (when its fixation
aligns with the actual anomaly distribution) but degrade specifically
when conditions change---the worst possible failure mode for a monitoring
system.

\subsection{Intervention Effectiveness (Run B)}
\label{sec:intervention}

We tested explicit intervention reminders on Claude Haiku. Due to API
authentication expiration, this experiment covers 64 ticks (1 seed)
rather than the full 200-tick session. Despite the truncated run, the
results are informative.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Intervention condition (Claude Haiku, 1 seed, 64 ticks).
Intervention reminders at tick 50 partially reduce directional fixation
and slow DA degradation compared to the baseline trajectory at the same
tick range.}
\label{tab:intervention}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Baseline (ticks 1--64)} & \textbf{Intervention
(ticks 1--64)} \\
\midrule
Starting DA        & 70\% & 80\% \\
DA at tick 64      & collapsing & 75\% \\
Fixation gap (DFG) & +42\% & +14\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Intervention reminders produced two effects: (1) DA started higher
(80\% vs.\ 70\% baseline) and remained higher at tick 64 (75\% vs.\
the baseline's already-collapsing trajectory), and (2) the directional
fixation gap was reduced from +42\% to +14\%. Fixation was not
eliminated---the model still showed some preference for early-phase
factors---but was substantially attenuated.

These results suggest that attention collapse in Claude Haiku is
partially responsive to explicit prompting, but that a structural
tendency toward fixation persists even with mitigation. Whether the
intervention effect would hold over the full 200-tick session remains an
open question given our truncated run.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}
% ============================================================

\subsection{What Explains the Cross-Model Divergence?}

The most salient result is the qualitative difference between Claude
Haiku and GPT-5.4. Several hypotheses merit consideration:

\paragraph{Model capacity.} Claude Haiku is a smaller model optimized
for speed and cost. It may lack the capacity to maintain coherent
multi-factor reasoning as context length grows. GPT-5.4, as a larger
model, may have more headroom for sustained attention distribution.
However, we note that GPT-5.4's \emph{absolute} DA is modest
($\sim$33--38\%), suggesting that stable performance is not the same as
\emph{good} performance. The larger model avoids collapse but does not
achieve high accuracy.

\paragraph{Training distribution.} The models may differ in how their
training data represents long-session monitoring tasks. If GPT-5.4's
training included more examples of sustained multi-factor analysis, it
might be better calibrated for the task even without achieving high
accuracy.

\paragraph{Context window management.} The two models may implement
different strategies for managing growing context windows. A model that
gives equal weight to all parts of the conversation history (including
early ticks where Load/Generation anomalies dominate) will naturally
exhibit directional fixation. A model that more aggressively discounts
older context may avoid this trap.

We emphasize that we cannot distinguish among these hypotheses with our
current data. The cross-model divergence is an empirical finding;
explaining its mechanistic cause is future work.

\subsection{The Invisible Collapse Zone}

The gap between FC = 1.00 and declining DA defines what we call the
\emph{invisible collapse zone}: the region where an agent appears to be
functioning correctly by format-level metrics but is making increasingly
wrong decisions. This zone has direct practical implications:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Monitoring dashboards} that track format compliance
    will not detect degradation.
    \item \textbf{Output validation} that checks structural completeness
    will pass.
    \item \textbf{Human reviewers} who skim responses for factor
    mentions may not notice that the recommended actions are wrong for
    the current conditions.
\end{itemize}

Only behavioral metrics---checking whether the agent's \emph{actions}
are appropriate given the \emph{current} state---can detect attention
collapse. This argues for behavioral evaluation as a mandatory component
of agent monitoring in production deployments.

\subsection{Directional Fixation as Availability Bias}

The directional fixation pattern we observe is consistent with what
cognitive science terms \emph{availability bias}
\cite{tversky1974judgment}: over-weighting information that is more
accessible in memory (or, in the LLM case, more prevalent in the
conversation history). Load and Generation anomalies dominate the first
80 ticks. By Phase 3, the conversation history contains far more
examples of Load/Generation anomalies than Weather/Reserve anomalies,
even though the \emph{current} tick features the latter.

This behavioral parallel is suggestive but does not constitute evidence
for a shared cognitive mechanism. The underlying cause in LLMs may be
prosaic---attention weight distributions that favor tokens appearing more
frequently in context \cite{vaswani2017attention}, or the well-documented
``lost in the middle'' effect \cite{liu2024lost} causing the model to
over-weight early and recent context at the expense of phase-transition
information.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Our study has several important limitations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Single scenario.} CoherenceBench currently uses one
    domain (power grid control). While we believe the underlying
    phenomenon generalizes, demonstrating this across diverse
    scenarios---medical triage, cybersecurity monitoring, financial
    surveillance---is necessary future work.

    \item \textbf{Two models.} We evaluate only Claude Haiku and
    GPT-5.4. Drawing conclusions about ``small vs.\ large models''
    from two data points would be premature. Other small models might
    not collapse; other large models might.

    \item \textbf{Truncated intervention run.} The intervention
    condition ran for only 64 ticks due to API authentication expiration.
    A full 200-tick intervention run with multiple seeds is needed to
    confirm the mitigation effect.

    \item \textbf{No architectural access.} We evaluate models as black
    boxes via their APIs. We cannot inspect attention weights,
    activations, or other internals. Our claims are therefore about
    \emph{behavioral} attention collapse, not about the underlying
    mechanism.

    \item \textbf{Ground truth scope.} Our acceptable-action sets are
    defined by domain heuristics, not by human expert consensus.
    Validation with power systems engineers would strengthen the
    benchmark's ecological validity.

    \item \textbf{Seed count.} Three seeds per condition is sufficient
    to observe the large effects we report (50 pp degradation) but
    insufficient for detecting smaller effects or computing tight
    confidence intervals.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Implications for Agent Evaluation}

The format--behavior dissociation we observe has immediate implications
for how the community evaluates long-running agents. Current practice
often relies on structural validation: does the output follow the
requested format? Does it mention the required components? Our results
show that this is insufficient. We recommend that agent benchmarks
include behavioral metrics that verify whether the agent's
\emph{decisions} are correct given the \emph{current} state, not
merely whether its \emph{outputs} are well-structured.

\subsection{Implications for Agent Architecture}

The partial effectiveness of intervention reminders (Section~\ref{sec:intervention})
suggests that attention collapse is not purely a context-window
phenomenon---if it were, a simple reminder should fully reset
performance. The persistence of residual fixation (+14\% DFG even with
intervention) suggests that something about the model's processing of
the accumulated conversation history resists override. This is
consistent with findings from the Engram project \cite{engram2026} on
coherence ceilings.

Future agent architectures might address this through explicit state
summarization (compressing the conversation history to prevent
historical anomaly distributions from dominating), factor-specific
sub-agents (dedicating separate processing to each monitored factor), or
adaptive context management (weighting recent ticks more heavily as the
session progresses).

\subsection{Implications for Deployment}

For practitioners deploying long-running agents in monitoring or
decision-support roles, our results suggest:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Do not rely on format compliance as a health check.}
    An agent that consistently produces well-structured outputs may
    still be making degraded decisions.
    \item \textbf{Implement behavioral spot-checks.} Periodically inject
    known anomalies and verify that the agent recommends appropriate
    actions.
    \item \textbf{Consider session length limits.} If attention collapse
    is session-length-dependent, shorter sessions with explicit state
    handoff may outperform continuous long sessions.
    \item \textbf{Model selection matters.} Different models exhibit
    qualitatively different coherence profiles. The best model for
    single-turn tasks may not be the best for long-session monitoring.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What We Do Not Claim}

We are careful to scope our claims. We do not claim that attention
collapse is a fundamental property of transformer architectures, that
all small models collapse, that all large models are immune, or that our
power grid scenario fully represents the space of long-running agent
tasks. We observe a measurable behavioral phenomenon in two specific
models under controlled conditions, and we provide a benchmark that
enables the community to test other models and scenarios.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We introduced CoherenceBench, a benchmark for measuring multi-factor
attention collapse in long-running autonomous agents. Our primary finding
is that format compliance and behavioral accuracy can diverge
dramatically: models maintain perfect format scores (FC = 1.00) while
Decision Accuracy degrades by up to 50 percentage points over 200 ticks.
This ``invisible collapse'' is undetectable by format-only evaluation.

Our cross-model comparison reveals qualitatively different coherence
profiles: Claude Haiku exhibits steep temporal degradation with strong
directional fixation (+38\% DFG), while GPT-5.4 maintains flat
performance with no directional bias. Explicit intervention reminders
partially mitigate collapse in Claude Haiku (reducing DFG from +42\% to
+14\%) but do not eliminate it.

These findings suggest that attention collapse is a model-dependent,
measurable phenomenon that existing benchmarks do not capture. As
autonomous agents are deployed in increasingly critical long-horizon
roles, behavioral coherence metrics like those in CoherenceBench should
become standard components of agent evaluation. CoherenceBench is
open-source and we invite the community to extend it with new scenarios,
models, and mitigation strategies.

\section{Reproducibility}
\label{app:reproducibility}
% ============================================================

CoherenceBench is designed for full reproducibility. Given a random seed,
the anomaly injection schedule, status data for all 200 ticks, and the
ground-truth acceptable action sets are deterministically generated. The
benchmark code, anomaly templates, and evaluation scripts are
open-source.

To reproduce our results:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Clone the CoherenceBench repository.
    \item Configure API keys for the target model.
    \item Run \texttt{python run\_bench.py --model <model> --seeds 3
    --ticks 200}.
    \item Evaluate with \texttt{python evaluate.py --results-dir
    <output>}.
\end{enumerate}

All experiments reported in this paper used temperature 0.0 and maximum
output tokens of 2048 per tick.
